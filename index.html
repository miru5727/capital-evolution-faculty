Primary Source · Required

Milton Friedman: "The Social Responsibility of Business Is to Increase Its Profits"

The New York Times Magazine, September 13, 1970

The essay that defined 50 years of corporate decision-making. Friedman argues that a corporate executive's only legitimate responsibility is to maximize profits for shareholders. Spending company money on social goals, he writes, is spending other people's money without their consent. This essay is the foundation of shareholder primacy; understand it before the course can complicate it.

What to watch for as you read

  • Friedman's core claim: only individuals have responsibilities, not corporations
  • His argument against CSR: executives who pursue social goals are "unelected public servants"
  • The nuance: Friedman does allow for long-term thinking and community investment within the profit mandate; it's his followers who stripped this out
  • The phrase "within the rules of the game". This is where Rule of Law enters the framework

Levine and MacBride: "The Frankenstein Economy"

The International Economy, Summer 2025

The essay that frames the book. Levine and MacBride argue that neoliberal capitalism has mutated into something its architects didn't intend. A system still animated by the spirit of profit but no longer tethered to the social contract. They introduce Dynamic Capitalism as the emerging response.

Key arguments

  • Business has overtaken government as the dominant force in American society, and that's a problem even if business is competent
  • CEO pay rose 900%+ from 1978–2018; worker pay rose modestly. Middle-income households shrank from 62% to 43% of U.S. household income
  • The "free market" is free only for the powerful; winner-take-all industries have replaced competition
  • Dynamic Capitalism is "neither a revolution nor socialism. It's people remembering what made American capitalism great in the first place"
  • The old neoliberal model told us wealth would trickle down. Dynamic Capitalism tells us it must circulate

Establishing Postwar Economic Norms

Bretton Woods (1944): The Postwar Foundation

In July 1944, representatives from 44 Allied nations met in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire and created the international monetary system that would govern the postwar world. The agreement established the U.S. dollar as the world's reserve currency, pegged to gold at $35 per ounce. All other currencies were pegged to the dollar, creating a stable exchange rate system that made international trade predictable and reliable.

Bretton Woods also created the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank. It held until 1971 when President Nixon ended dollar-gold convertibility: the "Nixon shock"; effectively ending the system. Its legacy: 25 years of stable, coordinated international finance that underpinned the Golden Age.

The Golden Age (1945–1973): When the Ladder Worked

The postwar period saw a broad consensus between government, labor, and business: when the economy grew, workers shared in it. Productivity grew at 3.3% per year on average; double the rate after 1973, and wages rose with it. The middle class expanded rapidly. New worker protections took hold: the 40-hour week, paid vacations, safety standards, minimum wage, and overtime.

A single factory job could carry a whole household: house, car, college, pension. That sounds almost made-up now. It was normal. The Golden Age wasn't golden for everyone; it excluded many Black, Indigenous, and immigrant communities, but it set mobility expectations that still echo in how your students think about what a degree and hard work should deliver.

The Friedman Doctrine and Shareholder Primacy: The Turn

In 1970, Milton Friedman published his New York Times essay arguing that a corporation's only legitimate purpose is to maximize returns to shareholders. An executive who spends company money on social goals is, in Friedman's framing, spending other people's money without their consent.

Shareholder primacy became the operating system of American business. Jack Welch at GE took it to its logical extreme: fire the bottom 10% annually, 600+ acquisitions, stock up ~25%/year. Wall Street celebrated. Then it collapsed; SEC fine, removed from the Dow in 2018. He's now the cautionary tale.

Key Terms

Bretton Woods
The 1944 international agreement that created a stable postwar monetary system anchored to the U.S. dollar. Held until 1971. Underpinned the Golden Age of coordinated global finance.
The Golden Age (1945–1973)
The postwar stretch of broad wage growth, expanding public investment, and a growing middle class, when productivity gains were widely shared across workers, owners, and communities.
Shareholder Primacy
The idea that a corporation's one legitimate purpose is maximizing returns to shareholders. All other considerations; workers, communities, environment; are secondary at best.
The Friedman Doctrine
Milton Friedman's 1970 argument that the social responsibility of business is to increase its profits. The ideological foundation of shareholder primacy. Note: Friedman was more nuanced than his followers.
Neoliberalism
A policy framework built on free markets, limited government, deregulation, and privatization. Made powerful by Reagan and Thatcher in the 1980s; adopted across the political spectrum by the 1990s.
Rule of Law
The foundational tenet underlying all of Dynamic Capitalism. That markets only work when everyone plays by the same rules, enforced fairly. Friedman himself included this: "within the rules of the game."

Why We Work a 5-Day Workweek

Dave Roos, History.com

The 40-hour week didn't arrive by accident. It took more than a century of labor organizing, wartime leverage, and ultimately an act of Congress (the Fair Labor Standards Act, 1938). Henry Ford adopted a 5-day week in 1926 not out of generosity but because mass production requires mass consumption; workers needed leisure time to buy things. That logic is a useful parallel to the Shared Prosperity tenet today.

Key facts

  • Ford adopted the 5-day week in 1926; other companies followed his lead
  • The 40-hour week became federal law in 1938 via the Fair Labor Standards Act
  • Frances Perkins, FDR's Secretary of Labor and first female cabinet member, was the driving force

What the Golden Age Built, and What It Left Behind

Who the Golden Age included, and didn't

Be direct about this: the prosperity was real, and it was unevenly shared. If you were Black, an immigrant, or a member of other marginalized communities, the era was often exclusionary and dangerous. Redlining denied Black families access to the GI Bill's homeownership benefits. Labor violence: the Ludlow and Elaine massacres; was part of the backdrop. The 40-hour week, when it came, didn't apply equally.

Hold both truths at once: the Golden Age functioned better for more people than today's economy does, and it still left many out. Teaching it honestly means naming that; not to diminish what was built, but to understand why the next version has to be built differently.

What it built that we still move through

The Golden Age didn't just grow the economy; it built the institutions your students still navigate. The GI Bill created the first generation of college-educated, home-owning working-class Americans. The Interstate Highway System connected the country. Federal R&D at ~10% of the budget; more than the rest of the world combined; funded ARPA, which became the internet. Social Security turned "getting old" from a financial catastrophe into a manageable life stage.

For faculty: a lot of what your discipline assumes about mobility, degrees, and what a career is supposed to deliver was built right here. The expectations your students carry into your classroom. That a degree leads somewhere, that hard work pays off; are Golden Age expectations. Understanding where they came from is part of understanding why they feel increasingly undeliverable.

The Cautionary Tale: Jack Welch at GE

Welch became CEO of GE in 1981 and ran shareholder primacy to its logical extreme. He fired the bottom 10% of his workforce every year as a matter of principle; earning the nickname "Neutron Jack" because he cleared people out and left the buildings standing. He completed 600+ acquisitions and cut around 250,000 jobs. The stock rose ~25% annually. Wall Street called him the greatest CEO of his generation.

Then the house of cards collapsed. GE paid a major SEC fine for accounting fraud. It was removed from the Dow Jones in 2018 after decades as a flagship American company. Welch is now the textbook cautionary tale: the full picture of what the Friedman Doctrine looks like when someone actually follows it all the way through.

The contrast with GE's earlier era is instructive: before Welch, GE's mission statement talked about the "balanced best interest of all"; customers, employees, shareholders, and the community. Welch threw that out. What we lost between those two versions of GE is the story of what changed after 1973.

The Three Tenets of Dynamic Capitalism

Dynamic Capitalism is built on three tenets; not a rejection of capitalism, but a framework for how markets should behave within a market economy. Each tenet is tested by the same rubric: is it structural (built into how the business works, not charity), long-term (stewardship over extraction), and authentic (costs something, not just PR)?

Tenet 1

Shared Prosperity

Economic structures and practices that distribute gains broadly, across workers, owners, and community, through how the business is built, not through charity. The heart of it: getting more people onto the equity side.

Tenet 2

Adaptive Institutions

Government and institutions that flex, measure, and partner; keeping markets fair and working; rather than dominating or disappearing. Neither laissez-faire nor statist: the smallest imposition necessary to get the desired outcome.

Tenet 3

Values-Driven Leadership

Leaders who make decisions anchored in something beyond quarterly returns, who understand their license to operate comes from society as much as from shareholders. The linchpin: Tenets 1 and 2 only work if leaders choose to use them.

The Throughline
Rule of Law
The foundational condition all three tenets depend on; markets only work when everyone plays by the same rules, enforced fairly. Friedman himself included it: "within the rules of the game."

What It's Not

Not Socialism

Dynamic Capitalism operates within market structures and private ownership. It challenges how markets behave; not whether they exist. No government ownership of industry, no redistribution by fiat.

Not ESG

ESG is a corporate measurement and reporting tool. A scoreboard. Dynamic Capitalism is a systemic framework for how economies and institutions are structured. One asks "how do we score?" The other asks "how do we build?"

Case Examples; The Tenets in Action

These cases are covered in Shannon's video. Each is run through the same rubric: structural, long-term, authentic, or charity and PR?

Tenet 1 · Shared Prosperity

Sexy Pizza (Denver)

Local Denver pizza company known for employee-first, profit-sharing practices. A Colorado example to test the rubric out loud: is the gain-sharing structural and ongoing, or a one-time perk?

Tenet 1 · Shared Prosperity

KKR / Pete Stavros & CHI Overhead Doors

When CHI sold in 2022, the average worker received ~$175,000. Broad employee ownership built into KKR's model across 55+ portfolio companies. Not altruistic; it works, which is why it spreads.

Tenet 2 · Adaptive Institutions

CHIPS Act

Federal investment to seed domestic semiconductor manufacturing. Government as early investor and enabler: the "fund foundational research, enable markets" idea in action. Good discussion prompt: adaptive or distorting?

Tenet 2 · Adaptive Institutions

Just Capital

Nonprofit that ranks America's largest companies on worker, community, and environmental treatment; surveys ~10,000 people per year. Worker pay tops its measurement factors. The scoreboard that holds business accountable.

Tenet 2 · Adaptive Institutions

Colorado Employee Ownership Office

A state office that helps businesses set up employee-ownership plans; lowering the biggest barrier: awareness andow-how. The book proposes this nationally; Colorado is the live local version.

Tenet 3 · Values-Driven Leadership

Patagonia / Yvon Chouinard

In 2022, the Chouinard family moved the entire company into a trust directing all profits to fighting climate change. "Earth is now our only shareholder." Values in the ownership structure itself; not a marketing campaign.

Primary Source · Required

Yvon Chouinard: "Earth Is Now Our Only Shareholder"

Patagonia, September 14, 2022

In September 2022, Chouinard transferred the entire company (valued at $3 billion) to two new entities: the Patagonia Purpose Trust and the Holdfast Collective. All profits go to fighting climate change. "I never wanted to be a businessman," he writes. The letter is short, direct, and structurally significant: this isn't a mission statement, it's a legal transfer. Values-Driven Leadership expressed in the ownership structure itself.

What to notice

  • "Instead of extracting value from nature and transforming it into wealth for investors, we'll use the wealth Patagonia creates to protect the source of all wealth."
  • Chouinard explicitly rejected selling or going public: "What a disaster that would have been. Even public companies with good intentions are under too much pressure to create short-term gain."
  • The structure is irrevocable; not a pledge, not a PR campaign. The Chouinard family gave up the conventional founder payday entirely.
  • Run it through the Module 3 rubric: structural? Long-term? Authentic? This is what three-for-three looks like.

Brook Eddy, Bhakti Chai

Boulder, Colorado · B Corp · Values-driven founder

Brook Eddy started Bhakti Chai in her Boulder kitchen in 2006, inspired by a trip to India studying a social movement built on the principle of Bhakti; devotion through social action.
