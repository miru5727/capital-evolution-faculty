The Economic Story Behind Your Odds

The coin flip your generation inherited

In 1940, about 90% of children went on to outearn their parents. For your generation, it is closer to a coin flip: roughly 50%. That is not a personal failure. It is the result of specific economic decisions made over 50 years that changed who benefits when the economy grows.

50%

individuals hold 50% of the world's wealth (Oxfam, 2017)

Understanding how we got here is not just economic history. It is the backstory of the job market you are entering, the debt you may be carrying, and the institutions you are betting on.

The Bretton Woods Foundation (1944)

After World War II, 44 nations met in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire and built a new international monetary system. They anchored all currencies to the U.S. dollar and the dollar to gold, creating stable exchange rates that made global trade predictable. This stability was the financial foundation for everything that followed. The system held until 1971 when President Nixon ended dollar-gold convertibility, but by then the postwar boom had already transformed American life.

The Golden Age (1945 to 1973): When the ladder actually worked

For roughly 30 years after World War II, the U.S. economy ran on a shared compact: when productivity grew, workers shared in it. One factory job could support a whole household. A single income could buy a house, a car, send kids to college, and end in a pension. That sounds almost made up now. It was normal.

The GI Bill sent a generation to college who never would have gone. The Interstate Highway System connected the country. Federal investment in research and development funded the projects that eventually became the internet. A 1958 Gallup poll found 73% of Americans had high confidence in the federal government, the highest ever recorded.

It was not golden for everyone. Black Americans, immigrants, and other marginalized communities were largely excluded through redlining, labor discrimination, and outright violence. Naming that is part of understanding it honestly. But the era set expectations about what work should deliver that your generation is still being compared to.

The Friedman Doctrine and the turn away from shared growth

In 1970, economist Milton Friedman published a New York Times essay arguing that a corporation's only responsibility is to maximize profits for shareholders. Spending company money on workers, communities, or anything else is, in his view, spending other people's money without their consent.

This idea, called shareholder primacy, became the operating logic of American business. It was made politically powerful by Reagan and Thatcher in the 1980s through deregulation, privatization, and market-first policy. By the 1990s both parties had adopted it. The result: productivity kept growing after 1973, but wages stopped tracking it. The economy grew. The gains stopped being shared.

What this means for you right now

The last wave of automation eliminated manufacturing jobs. The current wave, driven by artificial intelligence, is targeting entry-level white-collar work: the analyst role, the paralegal, the coordinator, the person who manages data and documents. The bottom rung of the career ladder your degree is supposed to get you onto is the rung being automated first.

That is not a reason to despair. It is a reason to understand the system well enough to navigate it and, eventually, to change it. That is what this course is building toward.

Capital Evolution: Golden Age and Unraveling Chapters

Seth Levine and Elizabeth MacBride (2025)

The primary source for this module. Levine and MacBride trace the postwar compact between government, labor, and business, and then the decade-by-decade unraveling after 1973. Read it alongside the video for the fullest picture.

What to focus on

  • The Treaty of Detroit (1950): when the autoworkers union and GM agreed that when the company wins, workers win too. That deal shaped two generations of working-class life.
  • Marion Folsom: a Kodak executive who helped build Social Security. Before it existed, a layoff could mean your family did not eat that month.
  • The Ludlow Massacre (1914): a Colorado mine strike that killed up to 199 people. The public outrage changed how Rockefeller thought about workers and funded the research that Folsom later built on.
  • The Kodak vs. Apple comparison: Kodak at its peak employed 145,000 people for $19 billion in sales. Apple in 2023 employed 161,000 people for $383 billion in revenue. The company can win enormously while creating far fewer middle-class jobs.

Porchlight / ChangeThis Essay

The core argument of the book in essay form. Levine and MacBride make the case that shareholder primacy has collapsed under its own weight, and that Dynamic Capitalism is the emerging alternative. Key arguments

  • Dynamic Capitalism re-democratizes economic power by broadening ownership and re-anchoring business in community

  • Capitalism and democracy are siblings. When one falters, both do.

The Three Tenets of Dynamic Capitalism

Dynamic Capitalism is built on three tenets. Not a rejection of capitalism, but a framework for how markets should behave within a market economy. The test for any company or leader: is it structural (built into how the business actually works, not charity), long-term (stewardship over short-term extraction), and authentic (it costs something, it is not just PR)?

Tenet 1

Shared Prosperity

Spread the gains. Get more people onto the equity side. The goal is economic structures that distribute what a company earns broadly: across workers, owners, and community, through how the business is built, not through charity.

Tenet 2

Adaptive Institutions

Government and institutions that flex, measure, and partner. Not dominate, not disappear. The smallest imposition necessary to get the desired outcome. Think: early investor in risky research that no private market would fund. Think: scoreboard that holds business accountable.

Tenet 3

Values-Driven Leadership

Leaders who make decisions anchored in something beyond quarterly returns. Their license to operate comes from society as much as from shareholders. This is the linchpin: tenets 1 and 2 only work if leaders actually choose to use them.

The Throughline
Rule of Law
All three tenets depend on markets where everyone plays by the same rules, enforced fairly. Friedman himself included it: "within the rules of the game."
Not Socialism

Dynamic Capitalism operates inside market structures and private ownership. It challenges how markets behave, not whether they exist. No government ownership of industry.

Not ESG

ESG is a measurement and reporting tool: a scoreboard. Dynamic Capitalism is a systemic framework for how economies are structured. One asks "how do we score?" The other asks "how do we build?"

The Tenets in Action

Tenet 1: Shared Prosperity
Sexy Pizza (Denver) and CHI Overhead Doors

When CHI sold in 2022, the average worker received about $175,000. Sexy Pizza is a local Colorado example of employee-first profit sharing. The test: is the gain-sharing structural and ongoing, or a one-time perk?

Tenet 2: Adaptive Institutions
Colorado Employee Ownership Office and Just Capital

Colorado has a state office that helps businesses set up employee-ownership plans. Just Capital surveys 10,000 people per year to rank major companies on how they treat workers. The scoreboard and the infrastructure to make ownership spread.

Tenet 3: Values-Driven Leadership
Patagonia and the Aurora Mayor

In 2022, the Chouinard family transferred Patagonia into a trust directing all profits to fighting climate change. "Earth is now our only shareholder." A local Colorado leader is doing the same kind of values-first work in public leadership. Module 3 goes deeper.

Activity 2: The Fork in the Road

Economic systems are not abstract: they show up in the choices you actually make. This activity puts you at a real decision point and walks you through where different paths lead. There are no trick endings. Both paths have real trade-offs. Pay attention to what surprises you.

You just graduated. Two job offers are on the table.

You have two weeks to decide. Both are real. Neither is obviously right.

Option A

Clearwater Collective

Mid-sized employee-owned company. Starting salary: $42,000. After two years you receive an ownership stake. Profits are shared quarterly. No-layoff policy. Executives earn no more than 5x the median worker wage.

Option B

Vantage Group

Regional subsidiary of a publicly traded corporation. Starting salary: $50,000. Clear promotion track. Executive bonuses tied to quarterly shareholder returns. No profit-sharing or ownership program.

You choose Clearwater Collective.

Your first year: you are taking home less than friends at larger firms. You are aware of it. But your onboarding includes a financial literacy session explaining how your ownership stake accumulates. For the first time, you understand the difference between a wage and equity. Now a choice:

Path A1

You lean in

You get involved in a worker council. You start to feel like your opinion shapes the company. By year three your ownership stake has real value.

Path A2

You struggle with the trade-off

The salary gap is real. A medical expense in year two puts pressure on your budget. You wonder if you made the right call.

Path A1: You Lean In

Financial Trajectory

Wages plus ownership distributions outpace the higher-salary path by year 10

You are building wealth, not just income. By the time a competitor offers you $60,000 to leave, you run the numbers and stay. The equity is worth more.

Work and Wellbeing

Ownership correlates with lower burnout and higher job satisfaction

Workers with ownership stakes consistently report more autonomy and more investment in outcomes. You feel the difference.

Community Impact

Clearwater's local sourcing keeps dollars in the regional economy

Your company is one your neighbors can name and benefit from. You are part of something visible.

The Honest Caveat

Ownership stakes take time to vest

The trade-off is real. Shared prosperity models only work if workers can afford to wait. That is why wages and equity have to move together, not one instead of the other.

Tenet 1: Shared Prosperity

Path A2: The Trade-Off Is Real

Financial Trajectory

The short-term squeeze is genuine

Ownership stakes take time to materialize. The trade-off is not imaginary: it requires stability and patience to pay off. Financial stress in early career is a real burden.

The Systemic Point

Shared prosperity models only work when workers can afford to wait

This is the design problem Dynamic Capitalism has to solve. You are experiencing it directly. The model is right, but the implementation has to account for real financial vulnerability.

What Does Not Change

You choose Vantage Group.

Your salary is higher and it shows immediately. Your performance reviews are strong. A promotion looks likely by year three. Now a choice:

Path B1

You move up

You are good at this. By year five you are managing a team. The promotion track is real. But decisions above you move fast and rarely include people below director level.

Path B1: You Move Up

Financial Trajectory

Strong in the short and medium term

But without equity or profit-sharing, your wealth accumulation depends entirely on salary increases and personal investing. That requires capital you may or may not have.

Work and Wellbeing

High-performance environments with low worker agency correlate with elevated burnout

A cost-cutting initiative eliminates two roles on your team. You were not consulted. The promotion track is real, but so is the pressure. The decisions that shape your work life are made without you.

Community Impact

The layoff that hit the company three years ago was felt in the community

It was not personal. But it was real. The supply chain you are part of carries an environmental cost that does not show up in your paycheck.

The Honest Tension

Good careers and real harm can coexist in the same company

The question is whether you have agency over which one you contribute to, and whether your leadership, if you get there, makes any difference to the structure.

When individual companies do not change on their own, the question becomes structural
